# The AI Transformation & General-Purpose Technology

<div class="glass-effect">

- AI rapidly transforms work, life, learning.
- Profound daily impact: loans, social media.
- Promise for growth, but raises job, ethics, equality questions.
- Transformative as past innovations (steam engine, electricity, internet).
- New industrial revolution: human-machine blurring.
- Driving Fourth Industrial Revolution.
- AI performs tasks faster, more accurately (manufacturing, services, agriculture).
- Over 50% organizations use AI; growing.
- Half of work tasks potentially automated by 2055.
- Intense debate on future of work.

</div>

## The Double-Edged Impact & Societal Ripple Effects

<div class="glass-effect">

- Technology displaces & creates jobs; AI follows.
    - Displaces jobs.
    - Creates new jobs, elevates work.
- Reshaping workplace, transforming job roles.
- New roles in data analysis, ML, AI ethics.
- Humans & machines collaborating, amplifying capabilities.
- **Opportunities:** Higher productivity, new services, better decisions.
- **Challenges:** Exacerbates inequalities (AI skills gap - look at lessons learned from the digital divide).
- Raises ethical dilemmas: data privacy, bias, accountability.

</div>

## Global AI Impact (Estimations): Workforce, Automation & Readiness (United States)


<div class="glass-effect">

•	Up to 30% of U.S. jobs could be fully automated by 2030, and 60% will experience significant task-level changes due to AI integration.

•	U.S. companies using ChatGPT report 23.5% replacing workers, and nearly half of ChatGPT users say it replaced some job functions.  

•	A 2024 survey shows 50% of U.S. workers believe AI improved their 
professional skills and nearly 70% use automation tools regularly.  

•	71% of U.S. employees worry about AI’s impact on their job, with 75% fearing obsolescence and 65% uneasy about AI replacement.  

</div>

## Automation and Job Market Alteration

<div class="elegant-box">

- AI is rapidly transforming labor markets.
- AI automation handles repetitive, routine tasks (manufacturing, data entry, customer service).
- McKinsey (2030): Up to 375M workers may need to switch occupations due to AI.
- Historically, tech both destroys and creates jobs; AI is similar.
- WEF: 85M jobs displaced by 2025, but 97M new jobs could emerge.
- Quantity of work may hold steady, but its nature is profoundly altered.
- "Hollowing out" of job market:
    - Decline in middle-skill, routine jobs.
    - Growth in high-skill tech roles (AI developers, data scientists).
    - Growth in low-skill service roles (care workers, hospitality).
- Tendency toward polarization: more jobs at top/bottom, fewer in middle.
- Middle-skill work is changing, not disappearing (e.g., electricians, technicians with AI tools).

</div>

## Transformation Examples & New Roles

<div class="elegant-box">

- **Construction Industry:**
    - Uses drones, AI design software, robotic bricklayers.
    - Workers shift to overseeing, programming, maintaining smart machines.
    - Human labor redefined: managing complex processes, interpreting AI outputs, nuanced decisions.
- **Customer Service:**
    - AI chatbots handle basic inquiries.
    - Human agents focus on complex, emotionally nuanced cases.
    - AI triages requests, provides suggested answers, detects sentiment.
    - Human role: problem-solving, empathy, relationship management.
- **Collaboration, Not Replacement:** AI as "co-workers" – augmenting human capabilities.
- **New Job Roles Emerging Directly from AI:**
    - AI ethicist, prompt engineer, MLOps, data annotator, human-AI interaction designer.
    - New specialties to build, maintain, and audit AI systems.
    - Traditional fields (healthcare, education, law) hiring AI consultants (e.g., medical AI analysts, learning technologists).
</div>

- Some jobs will be eliminated or diminished (e.g., drivers, cashiers).
- AI threatens white-collar jobs: writing reports, drafting contracts, data analysis.
- AI advances in writing/analysis put office jobs at risk (e.g., marketing copy, code drafts).
- Algorithmic scheduling: AI decides shifts, potentially making jobs precarious/stressful.
- Intense monitoring (AI surveillance) raises safety and privacy issues.
- AI's labor impact: not just quantity, but *which* jobs, *whose* jobs, and *under what conditions*.
- Educators must anticipate shifts, prepare people to thrive sustainably with rights and wellbeing.

<div style="display: flex; flex-direction: row; align-items: center; gap: 1px;">

## Key Essential Competencies: Critical Thinking and Problem-Solving

<div style="flex: 1;
            background: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.1);
            border: 1px solid rgba(255,255,255,0.3);

            border-radius: 20px;
            color: #004e92;
            font-weight: bold;
            box-shadow: 0 4px 20px rgba(0,0,0,0.2);">

- AI excels at defined questions, but lacks general reasoning in novel situations.
- Humans needed to analyze complex, ambiguous problems, challenge assumptions, ask right questions (especially about AI outputs).
- Essential skill: knowing when to trust vs. question an algorithm's result.
- Employers seek critical data interpretation and non-rule-based decision-making.
- Judgment (experience, ethics, critical analysis) is irreplaceable by machines.

## Key Essential Competencies: Fading Competencies

<div style="flex: 1;
            background: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.1);
            border: 1px solid rgba(255,255,255,0.3);
            backdrop-filter: blur(10px);
            padding: 1.5em;
            border-radius: 20px;
            color: #004e92;
            font-weight: bold;
            box-shadow: 0 4px 20px rgba(0,0,0,0.2);">

- Broadly, routine skills (manual or cognitive) are less in demand.
- Includes: rote memorization, basic computation, repetitive manufacturing tasks.
- Mental arithmetic less critical; understanding *which* calculation to ask for matters more.
- Physical skills in repetitive motions declining (robots handle them).
- Pure information retrieval partially automated by AI assistants.
- Narrow technical skills quickly become outdated; underlying conceptual skill counts.
- AI "co-pilots" write code; programmer's value shifts to oversight, higher-level design and debugging.
- Continuous learning of new tech more important than knowing any one tech.
- Advanced technical skills are in demand, but require human/soft skills too.
- **Sweet spot:** T-shaped skill set (depth in one field, breadth across many).

</div>

## Convergence on Essential Skills & Implications for Education

<div style="flex: 1;
            background: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.1);
            border: 1px solid rgba(255,255,255,0.3);
            backdrop-filter: blur(10px);
            padding: 1.5em;
            border-radius: 20px;
            color: #004e92;
            font-weight: bold;
            box-shadow: 0 4px 20px rgba(0,0,0,0.2);">

- Multiple sources emphasize critical thinking, creativity, digital literacy as must-haves.
- Reskilling programs and educational institutions must evolve to instill these skills.
- Employers value adaptability, problem-solving, creative thinking more than formal qualifications ("skills are the new currency").
- UNESCO: Transversal skills enable adaptation and job transfer in a shifting landscape.
- **Summary:** Thrive with AI by building human-centric skills: critical thinking, complex problem-solving, creativity, communication, collaboration, adaptability, ethical judgment.
- Purely routine skills or one-time learning are insufficient for a long career.
- Profound implications for designing education at all levels.

## Learning-Integrated Work & Systemic Support

<div style="background: linear-gradient(to right, #d0eaff, #f0faff);

- Forward-thinking organizations: internal academies, partnerships with educators.
- Industry's role: Public-private partnerships for up-to-date curricula and apprenticeships.
- Governments: Update policies to support lifelong learning (funding, incentives, flexible certification).

- Systemic supports are crucial; individuals cannot constantly retrain at their own expense.

## Equity Dimension of Learning Pathways

<div style="background: linear-gradient(to right, #d0eaff, #f0faff);

            border-radius: 20px;
            color: #2e4d1b;
            font-weight: bold;
            box-shadow: 0 0 15px rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.2);">

- Without deliberate action, AI could widen educational inequalities ("digital divide").
- Inclusion must be front and center in evolving education.
- Lifelong learning opportunities must be accessible to all: rural, marginalized, disabled.
- Means: community training centers, free online resources, assistive technologies.
- Universal quality basic education is the foundation.
- UNESCO-UNEVOC: Stresses "flexible pathways to lifelong learning opportunities for all."
- Goal: AI helps "elevate TVET for a just and sustainable future for all."

### 5.1. Updating Curricula and Programs

<div class="glass-effect">

- **Integrate AI Knowledge & Skills:**
    - Basic AI literacy and implications for all students.
    - Technical/Vocational (TVET): Curricula reflect industry digitalization (automation, 5G, IoT).
    - Examples: AI diagnostics in automotive, precision farming with AI.
- **Balance New Tech & Fundamentals:**
    - Students learn new technologies alongside enduring professional principles.
    - Digital tools seamlessly integrated, not isolated add-ons (e.g., digital blueprints with traditional reading).
    - Goal: Augmented competence – enhancing, not replacing, core professional skills.
- **Embed Transversal Skills:**
    - Cultivate critical thinking, problem-solving via inquiry-based learning, teamwork, interdisciplinary tasks.
    - Revamping national competency standards to explicitly include soft skills.
    - Building "transversal digital competencies" into vocational curricula.
- **AI as a Subject:**
    - Emerging introductory AI courses/modules in high schools.
    - Vocational short courses on AI basics for trades (e.g., "AI for automotive technicians").
    - AI ethics modules for all disciplines to raise societal awareness.

### 5.2. Empowering and Training Teachers

<div class="glass-effect">

- **Teachers as Linchpin:** Invest in professional development for confidence in teaching about/with AI.
- **UNESCO Framework (2024) - Teacher Competencies (among others):**
    -  Understanding AI basics.
    -  Using AI tools in teaching practice.
    -  Ethics of AI use.
- **Achieving Competencies:**
    - Offer training workshops/courses on AI in education (e.g., AI-driven learning analytics).
    - In-service teachers attend institutes/online courses on educational AI tools, limitations, ethics.
    - Emerging models: AI mentorship, "digital coaches" for integrating AI tools.
    - Encourage teacher experimentation with AI (e.g., chatbots for quizzes, AI simulations).
    - Create a safe environment for new methods; school leaders support pilots.
- **Teacher Education Institutions:**
    - Update pre-service curricula: data literacy, tech-enabled pedagogy, AI ethics.
    - Align certification with frameworks (e.g., European DigCompEdu AI supplements).
- **Core Pedagogical Principles Endure:**
    - Teachers still foster critical thinking, adapt to student needs.
    - AI assists by providing analytics, handling administrative burdens, freeing teachers for human-centric teaching.
    - Showcase AI's value (time-saving, enhanced learning outcomes).
- **Handling AI Challenges in Class:**
    - Train teachers on academic honesty with AI (e.g., ChatGPT for assignments).
    - Teach students to use AI as support, not a crutch.

{{1}} - **Students:** Drivers of change; demand relevant education, embrace lifelong learning, insist on ethical tech use.

</div>

{{3}} - Embrace tech, sharpen human strengths.
